					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Academic discipline concerned with creating literature</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the television episode, see <a href="/wiki/Creative_Writing_(My_Name_Is_Earl)" class="mw-redirect" title="Creative Writing (My Name Is Earl)">Creative Writing (My Name Is Earl)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Creative writing</b> is any writing that goes outside the bounds of normal <a href="/wiki/Professional_writing" title="Professional writing">professional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Journalistic">journalistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic_writing" title="Academic writing">academic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Technical_writing" title="Technical writing">technical</a> forms of literature, typically identified by an emphasis on narrative craft, character development, and the use of literary <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">tropes</a> or with various traditions of <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poetics" title="Poetics">poetics</a>. Due to the looseness of the definition, it is possible for writing such as <a href="/wiki/Feature_story" title="Feature story">feature stories</a> to be considered creative writing, even though it falls under journalism, because the content of features is specifically focused on narrative and character development. Both <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fictional</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fictional</a> works fall into this category, including such forms as <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biography" title="Biography">biographies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_stories" class="mw-redirect" title="Short stories">short stories</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poem" class="mw-redirect" title="Poem">poems</a>. In the academic setting, creative writing is typically separated into fiction and <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> classes, with a focus on writing in an original style, as opposed to imitating pre-existing genres such as <a href="/wiki/Crime_fiction" title="Crime fiction">crime</a> or <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror</a>. Writing for the screen and stage&#8212;<a href="/wiki/Screenwriting" title="Screenwriting">screenwriting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Playwright" title="Playwright">playwriting</a>&#8212;are often taught separately, but fit under the creative writing category as well.
</p><p>Creative writing can technically be considered any writing of <a href="/wiki/Originality" title="Originality">original</a> <a href="/wiki/Composition_(language)" title="Composition (language)">composition</a>. In this sense, creative writing is a more contemporary and process-oriented name for what has been traditionally called <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, including the variety of its <a href="/wiki/Literary_genre" title="Literary genre">genres</a>. In her work, <i>Foundations of Creativity</i>, Mary Lee Marksberry references Paul Witty and <a href="/wiki/Lou_L._LaBrant" title="Lou L. LaBrant">Lou LaBrant</a>'s <i>Teaching the People's Language</i> to define creative writing. Marksberry notes:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Witty and LaBrant...[say creative writing] is a composition of any type of writing at any time primarily in the service of such needs as
</p><ol><li>the need for keeping records of significant experience,</li>
<li>the need for sharing experience with an interested group, and</li>
<li>the need for free individual expression which contributes to mental and physical health.<sup id="cite_ref-Marksberry_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marksberry-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li></ol></blockquote>

<p>Unlike its academic counterpart of writing classes that teach students to compose work based on the rules of the <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, creative writing is believed to focus on students' self-expression.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> While creative writing as an educational subject is often available at some stages, if not throughout, <a href="/wiki/K%E2%80%9312_education" class="mw-redirect" title="K–12 education">K–12 education</a>, perhaps the most refined form of creative writing as an educational focus is in <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>. Following a reworking of university education in the <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">post-war</a> era, creative writing has progressively gained prominence in the university setting. In the UK, the first formal creative writing program was established as a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts degree</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_East_Anglia" title="University of East Anglia">University of East Anglia</a> in 1970 <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> by the novelists <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Bradbury" title="Malcolm Bradbury">Malcolm Bradbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angus_Wilson" title="Angus Wilson">Angus Wilson</a>. With the beginning of formal creative writing programs:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>For the first time in the sad and enchanting history of literature, for the first time in the glorious and dreadful history of the world, the writer was welcome in the academic place. If the mind could be honored there, why not the imagination?<sup id="cite_ref-Engle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs_of_study">Programs of study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creative_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Programs of study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Creative Writing programs are typically available to writers from the high school level all the way through graduate school/university and adult education. Traditionally these programs are associated with the English departments in the respective schools, but this notion has been challenged in recent time as more creative writing programs have spun off into their own department. Most Creative Writing degrees for undergraduates in college are <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">Bachelor of Fine Arts</a> degrees (BFA).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Some continue to pursue a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Fine_Arts" title="Master of Fine Arts">Master of Fine Arts</a> in Creative Writing, the terminal degree in the field. Once rare, <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> programs are becoming more prevalent in the field, as more writers attempt to bridge the gap between academic study and artistic pursuit.
</p><p>Creative writers often decide an emphasis in either fiction or poetry, and it is normal to start with short stories or simple poems. They then make a schedule based on this emphasis including literature classes, education classes and workshop classes to strengthen their skills and techniques. Though they have their own programs of study in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screenwriting" title="Screenwriting">screenwriting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Playwright" title="Playwright">playwriting</a> have become more popular in creative writing programs, since creative writing programs attempt to work more closely with film and theatre programs as well as English programs. Creative writing students are encouraged to get involved in extracurricular writing-based activities, such as publishing clubs, school-based literary magazines or newspapers, writing contests, writing colonies or conventions, and extended education classes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_classroom">In the classroom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creative_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In the classroom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Creative writing is usually taught in a <a href="/wiki/Writing_Workshop" title="Writing Workshop">workshop</a> format rather than <a href="/wiki/Seminar" title="Seminar">seminar</a> style. In workshops, students usually submit original work for peer critique. Students also format a writing method through the process of writing and re-writing. Some courses teach the means to exploit or access latent creativity or more technical issues such as <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narrative_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrative structure">structural</a> <a href="/wiki/Literary_technique" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary technique">techniques</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">genres</a>, random <a href="/wiki/Plot_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Plot generator">idea generating</a> or unblocking <a href="/wiki/Writer%27s_block" title="Writer&#39;s block">writer's block</a> . Some noted <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">authors</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_Chabon" title="Michael Chabon">Michael Chabon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazuo_Ishiguro" title="Kazuo Ishiguro">Sir Kazuo Ishiguro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Brockmeier" title="Kevin Brockmeier">Kevin Brockmeier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_McEwan" title="Ian McEwan">Ian McEwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kirchwey" title="Karl Kirchwey">Karl Kirchwey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-twsOctBeqqq_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twsOctBeqqq-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rose_Tremain" title="Rose Tremain">Dame Rose Tremain</a> and reputed screenwriters, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Benioff" title="David Benioff">David Benioff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darren_Star" title="Darren Star">Darren Star</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Farrelly" title="Peter Farrelly">Peter Farrelly</a>, have graduated from university creative writing programs.
</p><p>Many educators find that using creative writing can increase students' academic performance and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_resilience" title="Psychological resilience">resilience</a>. The activity of completing small goals consistently rather than unfinished big goals creates pride in one's brain, which exudes <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> throughout the brain and increases motivation. It has been shown to build resilience in students by documenting and analyzing their experiences, which gives the students a new perspective on an old situation and allows sorting of emotions. It also has been proven to increase a student's level of compassion and create a sense of <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> among students in what could otherwise be deemed an isolating classroom.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_in_academia">Controversy in academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creative_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Controversy in academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Creative writing is considered by some academics (mostly in the US) to be an extension of the <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a> discipline, even though it is taught around the world in many languages. The English discipline is traditionally seen as the critical study of literary forms, not the creation of literary forms. Some academics see creative writing as a challenge to this tradition. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, as well as increasingly in the US and the rest of the world, creative writing is considered a discipline in its own right, not an offshoot of any other discipline.
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To say that the creative has no part in education is to argue that a university is not universal.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Those who support creative writing programs either as part or separate from the English discipline, argue for the academic worth of the creative writing experience. They argue that creative writing hones the students' abilities to clearly express their thoughts and that creative writing entails an in-depth study of literary terms and mechanisms so they can be applied to the writer's work to foster improvement. These critical analysis skills are further used in other literary studies outside the creative writing sphere. Indeed, the process of creative writing, the crafting of a thought-out and original piece, is considered by some to constitute experience in creative <a href="/wiki/Problem-solving" class="mw-redirect" title="Problem-solving">problem-solving</a>.
</p><p>Despite a large number of academic creative writing programs throughout the world, many people argue that creative writing cannot be taught. Essayist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Menand" title="Louis Menand">Louis Menand</a> explores the issue in an article for the <i>New Yorker</i> in which he quotes <a href="/wiki/Kay_Boyle" title="Kay Boyle">Kay Boyle</a>, the director of the creative writing program at San Francisco State University for sixteen years, who said, "all creative-writing programs ought to be abolished by law."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Contemporary discussions of creative writing at the university level vary widely; some people value MFA programs and regard them with great respect, whereas many MFA candidates and hopefuls lament their chosen programs' lack of both diversity and genre awareness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_prisons">In prisons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creative_writing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In prisons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 1960s, American prisons began implementing creative writing programs due to the prisoner rights movement that stemmed from events such as the <a href="/wiki/Attica_Prison_riot" title="Attica Prison riot">Attica Prison riot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The creative writing programs are among many art programs that aim to benefit prisoners during and after their time in prison. Programs such as these provide education, structure, and a creative outlet to encourage rehabilitation. These programs' continuation relies heavily on volunteers and outside financial support from sources such as authors and activist groups.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Poets Playwrights Essayists Editors and Novelists, known as <a href="/wiki/PEN_America" title="PEN America">PEN</a>, were among the most significant contributors to creative writing programs in America. In 1971, PEN established the Prison Writing Committee to implement and advocate for creative writing programs in prisons throughout the U.S. The PEN Writing Committee improved <a href="/wiki/Prison_libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="Prison libraries">prison libraries</a>, inspired volunteer writers to teach prisoners, persuaded authors to host workshops, and founded an annual literary competition for prisoners. Workshops and classes help prisoners build self-esteem, make healthy social connections, and learn new skills, which can ease prisoner <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_reentry" title="Prisoner reentry">reentry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Creative writing programs offered in juvenile correction facilities have also proved beneficial. In Alabama, <i>Writing Our Stories</i> began in 1997 as an anti-violence initiative to encourage positive self-expression among incarcerated youths. The program found that the participants gained confidence, the ability to empathize and see their peers in a more positive light, and motivation to want to return to society and live a more productive life.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One California study of prison fine arts programs found art education increased emotional control and decreased disciplinary reports. Participation in creative writing and other art programs result in significant positive outcomes for the inmates' mental health, relationship with their families, and the facility's environment. The study evidenced improved writing skills enhanced one's ability in other academic areas of study, portraying writing as a fundamental tool for building one's intellect.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Teaching prisoners creative writing can encourage literacy, teach necessary life skills, and provide prisoners with an outlet to express regret, accountability, responsibility, and a kind of restorative justice.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
